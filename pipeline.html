These are the dishes I am testing right now. None of them are orderable yet, and some of them will not make the cut. That is what this page is for.

Everything here earned its spot the same way: it has to be as good on Wednesday as it was the day I cooked it, or better. Most of these are dishes that tradition says you are supposed to make ahead. Some have open questions I have not answered yet, whether the smoke holds three days, whether a glaze weeps in the bag, whether a packet of dried mushroom and kombu actually flavors your rice water. When one fails, it comes off this list.

Tea-Smoked Chicken, Brown Butter Dashi Grits, Alabama White Sauce
Sichuan · Japan · France · Alabama · Texas
Smoked chicken thighs over the creamiest grits you have ever eaten, with a cold, tangy white barbecue sauce poured over the top. The smoke happens in a pot on the stove, not a smoker, using tea leaves and sugar, which is how Sichuan cooks have done it for centuries. The grits are cooked with a Japanese seaweed broth that you will not taste as Japanese, you will just taste grits that are somehow deeper than grits should be. The white sauce is an Alabama thing, mayonnaise and vinegar and black pepper, and it is what people from north Alabama put on smoked chicken instead of red barbecue sauce. It is strange the first time and mandatory every time after.
Still testing: whether day three beats day one on the smoke.
Suya Flank Steak, Coconut Rice, Cucumber Kachumbari
Nigeria · Kenya · Vietnam · Texas
Suya is Nigerian street meat, and the whole trick is the crust: ground peanuts, ginger, and cayenne rubbed onto beef so thick that it toasts into a savory, nutty, spicy shell when it hits the pan. It sits on coconut rice, and next to it is kachumbari, the sharp cucumber and red onion salad Kenyans eat with grilled meat, cold and acidic and there to cut everything else. The dark drizzle over the top is burnt sugar and tamarind, sweet-sour and almost bitter. Peanut crust, coconut fat, cold acid, burnt sugar. Nobody in this city is making this.
Contains: Peanuts, heavily. This one is a hard no if you have a peanut allergy.
Kabocha Char Siu, Shiitake-Dashi Rice, Pickles
Canton · Italy · Japan · Vegetarian
Kabocha squash roasted in wedges and lacquered with Chinese barbecue glaze, the same glaze that goes on char siu pork, plus the fennel and rosemary and garlic that go on Italian porchetta. Squash wearing pork's entire wardrobe. It sits on rice you cook with a mushroom and seaweed packet that goes in the water, and the pickles on the side are there to cut the sweetness. It is vegetarian, and it is not a vegetarian accommodation, it carves and eats like a roast.
Still testing: the rice packet, and whether the lacquer holds three days in the bag.
Kare-Kare with XO Sauce
Philippines · Hong Kong · West Africa · Texas Gulf
Filipino oxtail stew in a toasted peanut sauce. Peanut sauce on beef sounds like a dare and eats like a hug. The oxtail cooks until the meat gives up and the collagen turns the whole thing glossy, and the sauce is thickened with ground toasted rice and ground peanuts, no flour, no cream. Long beans and eggplant go in because that is how it is done. On the side is XO sauce, a Hong Kong luxury condiment made of dried shrimp, ham, and chile, and it is what you add when you want the whole bowl to get louder. Made for rice.
Contains: Peanuts, Shellfish (dried shrimp in the XO).
Khoresh-e Gheimeh, Persian Dried Lime Braise
Iran
A Persian stew of beef and yellow split peas, and the thing that makes it is the dried limes. Whole limes are boiled and dried until they go hard and black inside, which ferments them, and then they get pierced and dropped in the pot where they slowly leak out a sourness that has no equivalent in Western cooking. It is not lemon. It is sour and bitter and funky all at once, savory rather than bright. There is no sweetness in this dish anywhere. The split peas melt down and round it out from the inside. Served over saffron rice, which is there to frame the sourness, not fight it. Most people have never eaten anything like this.
Fesenjan, Walnut and Pomegranate Braise
Iran · Georgia · Mexico
Persian chicken braised in a sauce made almost entirely of ground walnuts and pomegranate molasses. It cooks until the walnut oil breaks out and the sauce turns from purple to nearly black. It tastes sour, deeply savory, faintly sweet, and rich in a way that reads almost like chocolate but is not. Nobody guesses what is in it. Iranians make it the day before, always, because the walnut oil needs the time to separate and come back together and the molasses needs the time to get into the meat. A day-one fesenjan is a rough draft. Yours would be served on day three, which is when it is right.
Contains: Tree nuts (walnuts).
Sauerbraten Beef Cheeks, Caraway-Juniper Potatoes, Braised Red Cabbage
Germany
German pot roast, except made with beef cheeks, which are the best braising cut on the animal and almost nobody sells them. The beef sits in a vinegar and wine marinade for four days before it ever hits heat, and that is not a shortcut or a make-ahead trick, that is the recipe. Sauerbraten has been a four-day dish for centuries. The sauce gets thickened with crushed gingersnaps, which sounds insane and is exactly right, and there are dried sour cherries in it that keep the sourness from being sharp. Red cabbage on the side, cooked down with apple and vinegar for an hour until it collapses. Potatoes in a bag with caraway and juniper.
Contains: Gluten (gingersnaps), Dairy (butter, in the potato bag).
Yogurt-Braised Lamb, Alkaline Noodles, Aleppo-Mint Brown Butter
Persia · India · Japan · Turkey
Lamb shoulder braised in yogurt until it falls apart. The yogurt breaks on purpose during the cook and then comes back together, which is why the sauce is tangy and thick and completely stable, it already did the thing you would be afraid of it doing. Over ramen-style noodles, which are made with an alkaline salt that keeps them chewy where a normal noodle would go to mush. At the table you pour hot brown butter with Aleppo pepper and dried mint over the whole thing and it pools into the sauce. Charred scallions on top, blackened hard, because burnt is a flavor that never fades.
Contains: Dairy (yogurt, butter), Gluten (noodles).
Nixtamal Grits, Braised Greens, Country Ham, Bottarga
Mexico · American South · Italy
Grits, but made from corn that has been treated with lime the way tortilla corn is, which changes the corn completely and makes it taste rounder and deeper than any grits you have had. Braised collards underneath, country ham through it, and then the strange part: bottarga, which is cured fish roe, grated over the top like parmesan. It is salty and deep and it does the same thing anchovy does in the chili, which is make you wonder what is in it without ever tasting like fish. This is a Southern dish that took a detour through Mexico and Italy and came back better.
Contains: Fish (bottarga), Dairy (butter).
Umeboshi and Charred Preserved Lemon Chicken, Negi-Shio, Blistered Shishitos
Morocco · Japan · Sichuan
Two cultures figured out that if you pack a whole fruit in salt for a month, it turns into something else entirely. Morocco did it with lemons. Japan did it with plums. They are the same idea and they taste nothing alike, so this dish uses both. The preserved lemon gets burned in a screaming hot wok first, which nobody does, and it turns from bright and floral into dark and bitter. The umeboshi brings a sourness that is almost meaty. Over the chicken goes a scallion oil built on that same burnt lemon, and the shishito peppers on the side get blistered hard and stay crisp. Plain rice, because everything else on the plate is already shouting.
Heads up: about one shishito in ten is genuinely hot. Fun or a problem, depending on you.
Two-Garum Pasta: Gulf Shrimp, Colatura, Fish Sauce, Garlic Confit
Amalfi · Vietnam · Rome · Texas Gulf
Pasta with shrimp, garlic, chile, and olive oil, and the sauce is built on two different fermented fish sauces from opposite sides of the planet. Colatura is what southern Italians make from salted anchovies, and it is the direct descendant of the fish sauce ancient Rome ran on. Vietnamese nuoc mam is the same thing, salted fish fermented into liquid, invented on the other side of the world with no knowledge of the first. Nobody uses both because one is Italian and one is Vietnamese, but they are the same ingredient, and the Italian one is clean and round while the Vietnamese one is funkier and deeper. Together they cover a range neither covers alone. It does not taste like fish. It tastes like the best version of garlic and oil pasta you have had.
Contains: Shellfish (shrimp), Fish (colatura, fish sauce), Gluten (pasta).
Shrimp and Grits: Étouffée and XO
Louisiana · Hong Kong · Japan · Texas Gulf
Shrimp and grits, except two cuisines got asked the same question and gave different answers, and this dish uses both. Louisiana's answer is étouffée: make a stock from the shells, build a roux, smother the shrimp in it. Hong Kong's answer is XO sauce: take shrimp, dry them, and concentrate them with cured ham and chile into a jar. One is fresh, one is aged, and it is the same animal in two states of time. The grits underneath are cooked with a Japanese seaweed broth that you will never identify, you will just notice the grits are deeper than grits should be.
Contains: Shellfish (shrimp, dried shrimp), Gluten (roux), Dairy (butter).
Collard Saag, Seared Halloumi, Rice
Punjab · American South · Cyprus · Vegetarian
Saag is the Punjabi dish of greens cooked down for hours with ginger, garlic, and chile until they collapse into something dark and silky. It is traditionally made with mustard greens, and collards are botanically a mustard, which means the South has been growing India's green the whole time without either side noticing. The cheese is halloumi instead of paneer, because both are the two cheeses on earth engineered not to melt in a pan, but halloumi is brined and salty where paneer is blank. You sear it yourself, and it is impossible to get wrong, the cheese physically cannot melt.
Contains: Dairy (halloumi, ghee).
Pork Tenderloin, Double Dark Vinegar Agrodolce, Seared Polenta Cakes
Sicily · Modena · Zhenjiang · France
Pork with a dark sweet-sour onion sauce, and the sauce is built on two aged vinegars that have never been in the same kitchen. Balsamic comes from Modena and is grape-based, woody, and round. Chinkiang black vinegar comes from Zhenjiang and is rice-based, malty, and smoky. They are the same invention, a dark vinegar aged into sweetness, arrived at independently on two continents. Blended, they cover a range neither has alone. Under it, polenta that has been set overnight into a slab and cut into cakes, which you sear yourself into something crisp on the outside and molten in the middle.
Still testing: whether the polenta slab holds three days, and whether your pan really gets that molten center.
Contains: Dairy (butter, parmesan).

Why These And Not Others

Roughly forty dishes got cut to get to this list. The ones that survived all have the same thing in common: they use time as an ingredient instead of fighting it. The fesenjan needs days to darken. The sauerbraten's four-day marinade is the recipe, not a shortcut. The smoke on the chicken rounds off overnight. None of these tolerate being made ahead. They require it.
